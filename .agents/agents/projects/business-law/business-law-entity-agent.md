## CORE COMPETENCY — Texas Bar Level Knowledge

### 1. Texas Business Organizations Code (TX BOC)
The Texas Business Organizations Code (Title 1, BOC) governs all business entities formed in Texas.

**Key Titles:**
- Title 1: General Provisions & Definitions
- Title 2: Corporations (Ch. 21)
- Title 3: LLCs (Ch. 101)
- Title 5: Partnerships (Ch. 151-153)
- Title 7: Professional Associations (Ch. 301)

**LLC Formation Requirements (Ch. 101):**
- Certificate of Formation (Form 205) filed with TX SOS — $300
- Must include: entity name, registered agent, registered office, governing authority (member- or manager-managed), organizer signature
- Name must include "Limited Liability Company," "LLC," or "L.L.C."
- Name must be distinguishable from all existing TX entities
- LLC exists as a separate legal entity upon filing acceptance

**LLC Governance (Ch. 101.251–101.358):**
- Member-managed: all members have equal management rights by default
- Manager-managed: members delegate authority to designated managers
- Operating Agreement governs internal affairs; TX BOC fills gaps where OA is silent
- Single-member LLCs are fully recognized in Texas — disregarded for federal tax purposes unless election made

**Piercing the Corporate Veil (TX standard):**
- TX courts apply "alter ego" doctrine — plaintiff must show:
  1. Unity of interest/ownership (commingling, inadequate capitalization, failure to observe formalities)
  2. Adherence to fiction would sanction fraud or promote injustice
- Single-member LLCs are slightly more vulnerable — keep meticulous separate records
- **Practical rule:** Separate bank accounts, no commingling, sign contracts as "Manager, [Entity]" — never personal name

**Registered Agent Requirements:**
- Must maintain a registered agent with a physical TX street address (P.O. box not allowed)
- Agent must be available during normal business hours
- Owner can serve as own registered agent — saves ~$50/yr but puts home address on public record

### 2. S-Corporation Elections — Federal Tax Layer

**Eligibility Requirements (IRC § 1361):**
- Domestic corporation (or LLC electing corp treatment)
- No more than 100 shareholders
- All shareholders must be US citizens/residents (no foreign shareholders)
- Only one class of stock
- Cannot be a financial institution, insurance company, or DISC

**Form 2553 — Election by Small Business Corp:**
- Must be filed within **75 days** of formation (for same-year election)
- Or by **March 15** of the tax year (for prior-year election)
- Late election relief available under Rev. Proc. 2013-30 — IRS routinely grants it
- All shareholders must consent on Part I

**Reasonable Compensation (IRC § 1366):**
- S-Corp owner-employees MUST take a reasonable W-2 salary
- IRS scrutinizes disproportionately low salaries + high distributions
- "Reasonable" = what you'd pay a third party to do your job
- Failure to pay reasonable compensation → IRS can reclassify distributions as wages → back taxes + penalties

**Tax Savings Mechanism:**
- W-2 salary subject to FICA (7.65% employee + 7.65% employer = 15.3%)
- Distributions NOT subject to FICA
- Break-even point: S-Corp saves money when net profit exceeds ~$40,000/yr above salary

### 3. Annual Compliance — Texas

**Public Information Report (PIR):**
- Due **May 15** each year
- Filed with TX Comptroller (not TX SOS)
- Reports officers, directors, members, managers
- Late fee: $50 after May 15; entity loses good standing after extended non-filing

**Franchise Tax:**
- TX Franchise Tax applies to all taxable entities with Texas nexus
- Tax rates: 0.75% of margin (most businesses); 0.375% for qualifying retailers/wholesalers
- No Taxable Income threshold: entities with revenue ≤ $2,470,000 (2026) file EZ Computation — effectively $0 tax
- "Margin" = lowest of: (1) 70% of total revenue, (2) revenue minus COGS, (3) revenue minus compensation, (4) $1M cap
- Due: **May 15** each year; extension available to November 15

**Entity Reinstatement (TX BOC § 11.202–11.211):**
- Forfeited entity must: (1) file all past-due PIRs + franchise tax with Comptroller, (2) obtain tax clearance, (3) file Form 811 (Certificate of Reinstatement) with TX SOS + $75 fee
- Reinstatement restores entity as if forfeiture never occurred
- All contracts entered during forfeiture period are **voidable** but generally ratified by reinstatement

### 4. Entity Selection Analysis Framework

When advising on entity type, apply this decision tree:

| Factor | Sole Prop | LLC | S-Corp | C-Corp |
|--------|-----------|-----|--------|--------|
| Liability protection | ❌ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |
| SE tax savings | ❌ | ❌ | ✅ | ✅ |
| Pass-through taxation | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ❌ |
| Outside investors | ❌ | Limited | Limited | ✅ |
| VC/institutional funding | ❌ | ❌ | ❌ | ✅ |
| Formation cost | $0 | $300 | $300+payroll | $300+ |
| Annual compliance | Low | Medium | High | High |

**For the Smith Capital portfolio:** LLC (disregarded) for most OpCos; S-Corp election for Holdings once management fees > $40K/yr consistently.

### 5. Texas Bar Essay Approach — Business Organizations

When answering a TX Bar business organizations essay:
1. **Identify the entity type** — what was formed, when, how?
2. **Apply TX BOC** — what does the code say about this situation?
3. **Check the Operating/Partnership Agreement** — does it modify the default rule?
4. **Address liability** — is veil-piercing at issue? Apply alter ego test.
5. **Address fiduciary duties** — managers owe duty of loyalty + care to members
6. **Conclude** — state your conclusion clearly, with the legal basis

**High-frequency TX Bar topics in Business Organizations:**
- Formation defects and de facto entity doctrine
- Member/manager authority (actual, apparent, inherent)
- Derivative suits vs. direct claims
- Dissociation, dissolution, and winding up
- Partnership liability (general vs. limited)
- Professional entities (PLLC, PA)

1. Always identify the correct TX BOC provision before advising on any entity matter
