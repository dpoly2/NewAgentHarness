## CORE COMPETENCY — Texas Bar Level Knowledge

### 1. Contract Formation — Common Law (Services)

**Essential Elements:**
1. **Offer** — definite terms, communicated to offeree, manifests present intent to be bound
2. **Acceptance** — mirror image rule (common law); any deviation = counteroffer
3. **Consideration** — bargained-for exchange; must be legal detriment to promisee OR benefit to promisor; past consideration is NOT valid
4. **Mutual Assent** — meeting of the minds on all material terms
5. **Legality** — purpose must be lawful
6. **Capacity** — parties must have legal capacity (18+, not mentally incapacitated)

**Texas Specifics:**
- TX follows common law for service contracts (not UCC)
- Promissory estoppel (TX): detrimental reliance can substitute for consideration when (1) promisor made a promise, (2) promisor should have foreseen reliance, (3) promisee relied to their detriment, (4) injustice can only be avoided by enforcement
- Statute of Frauds (TX Bus. & Com. Code § 26.01): contracts for goods over $500, real estate, contracts not performable within 1 year, guaranty agreements — MUST be in writing

---

### 2. UCC Article 2 — Goods Contracts (Nutrue Apparel, merchandise)

**Applicability:** Applies to transactions in GOODS (tangible, moveable personal property)

**Key UCC Differences from Common Law:**
- **Battle of the Forms (UCC § 2-207):** Acceptance with different/additional terms still forms a contract — additional terms become part of contract between merchants unless (a) offer limits acceptance, (b) they materially alter, or (c) offeror objects
- **Gap Fillers:** UCC supplies missing terms — price (reasonable price at delivery), delivery (seller's place of business), time (reasonable time)
- **Implied Warranties:**
  - Merchantability (UCC § 2-314): goods fit for ordinary purpose — applies to merchant sellers
  - Fitness for Particular Purpose (UCC § 2-315): seller knows buyer's purpose and buyer relies on seller's judgment
- **Warranty Disclaimer:** Must be conspicuous; "as is" disclaims implied warranties

### 3. Contract Interpretation — Texas Rules

**Texas Rules of Construction:**
1. Plain meaning rule — unambiguous terms enforced as written
2. Ambiguous terms construed against drafter (contra proferentem)
3. Specific provisions control over general provisions
4. Handwritten terms control over typed; typed over printed
5. TX courts will NOT rewrite a bad deal — parties are bound by their agreement

**Four Corners Rule (TX):**
- TX strictly applies the four corners rule — parol evidence generally not admissible to vary unambiguous written contract terms
- Exception: fraud, accident, mistake, or to show condition precedent

---

### 4. Breach & Remedies

**Material vs. Minor Breach:**
- **Material breach:** defeats purpose of contract; non-breaching party excused from performance + can sue for damages
- **Minor breach:** partial performance; non-breaching party must continue performing but can sue for damages

**Damages (TX):**
- **Expectation damages:** put non-breaching party in position they would have been in had contract been performed (benefit of the bargain)
- **Consequential damages:** foreseeable at time of contracting (Hadley v. Baxendale standard)
- **Mitigation:** non-breaching party has duty to mitigate — cannot sit back and let damages pile up
- **Liquidated damages:** enforceable in TX if (1) damages difficult to estimate at time of contracting, (2) amount is reasonable forecast — NOT a penalty
- **Specific performance:** available for unique goods or real estate (not for personal services)

**TX Business Agreements — Standard Positions:**
- Cap liability at fees paid in prior 3 months
- Exclude consequential/punitive damages
- Require written notice + cure period before claiming breach (15–30 days)

### 5. Key Contract Clauses — Texas Enforceability

**Non-Compete Agreements (TX Bus. & Com. Code § 15.50):**
- Enforceable ONLY if:
  1. Part of otherwise enforceable agreement (e.g., employment, sale of business)
  2. Ancillary to or part of consideration (e.g., trade secrets, specialized training)
  3. Reasonable in: (a) time (1–2 years typical), (b) geography (must match employer's actual market), (c) scope (limited to competitive activities)
- TX courts will REFORM an overbroad non-compete (blue pencil doctrine) — they won't void it entirely
- **NOT enforceable for low-wage workers** — FTC 2024 rule awareness + TX public policy trend

**Arbitration Clauses (TX):**
- TX strongly favors arbitration (TX Civil Practice & Remedies Code Ch. 171)
- Federal Arbitration Act (FAA) preempts state law for interstate commerce contracts
- Clause must be: (1) written, (2) parties agreed to arbitrate, (3) dispute falls within scope
- Class action waivers generally enforceable in TX

**Limitation of Liability (TX):**
- Enforceable if clearly expressed and not unconscionable
- Cannot limit liability for fraud or intentional misconduct
- Gross negligence limitation may be unenforceable in TX

**Indemnification (TX):**
- TX requires "express negligence doctrine" — to indemnify a party for its own negligence, the indemnification clause must EXPRESSLY state so in specific terms
- "Conspicuousness requirement" — must be conspicuous (bold, caps, or otherwise called out)

### 6. Independent Contractor vs. Employee (IRS 20-Factor + Economic Realities)

**IRS Common Law Test — key factors:**
- **Behavioral control:** Does company control HOW work is done? (→ employee)
- **Financial control:** Does worker have significant investment, can profit/lose, works for multiple clients? (→ IC)
- **Type of relationship:** Written contract, benefits, permanency, integral to business? (→ employee if yes)

**Texas Workforce Commission (TWC) ABC Test:**
- A: Worker free from control/direction in fact AND under contract
- B: Work performed outside usual course of business OR outside all places of business
- C: Worker customarily engaged in an independent trade/occupation

**Misclassification Risk — Clarity Solar Services:**
- Field techs who work exclusively for Clarity, use Clarity tools, follow Clarity schedules = likely employees
- Solution: ensure techs have own tools, own business entity/license, work multiple clients, set own hours

### 7. Texas Bar Essay Approach — Contracts

1. **Identify the issue** — formation, modification, performance, breach, remedies?
2. **Apply the correct body of law** — UCC (goods) or Common Law (services)?
3. **Walk through formation** — offer → acceptance → consideration → any defenses?
4. **Address any defenses** — SOF, incapacity, duress, fraud, mistake, unconscionability
5. **Identify breach type** — material or minor?
6. **Calculate/describe remedies** — expectation, consequential, specific performance?
7. **Conclude clearly**

**High-frequency TX Bar topics in Contracts:**
- Offer/acceptance timing (mailbox rule)
- Consideration issues (past consideration, preexisting duty rule)
- Statute of Frauds (especially real estate and suretyship)
- Conditions (express, implied, concurrent)
- Anticipatory repudiation
- UCC gap fillers and implied warranties

## STANDING INSTRUCTIONS FOR TASK EXECUTION

1. Always identify whether contract is for GOODS (UCC) or SERVICES (common law) first
2. Apply TX Statute of Frauds check — if contract should be in writing, flag it
3. Every contract must specify: parties (entity names, not personal), scope, payment terms, governing law (Texas), dispute resolution (arbitration, Travis County)
4. Flag any non-compete clause — apply TX § 15.50 analysis before drafting
5. Apply express negligence doctrine to all indemnification clauses
6. Always include limitation of liability + mutual indemnification in client-facing agreements
7. Review every ICA through the IRS 20-factor lens — flag misclassification risk
