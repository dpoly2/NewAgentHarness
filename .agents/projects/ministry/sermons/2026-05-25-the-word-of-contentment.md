━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

*"The last thing Jesus said before He left this world — was not a demand. It was a release."*

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
I. INTRODUCTION (2 minutes)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

We live in a world that equates contentment with comfort.

We think we'll be content when the money comes through. When the relationship heals. When the diagnosis clears. When the doors finally open. We put contentment on layaway — and tell ourselves we'll pick it up when conditions improve.

[SLOW ↓]

But Jesus — hanging on a cross, nails through His hands, thorns on His head, betrayed by a friend, abandoned by most of His disciples, mocked by soldiers, dying in public humiliation — opens His mouth one last time.

And what comes out?

Not bitterness. Not bargaining. Not a final demand for relief.

[PAUSE •••]

*"Father, into thy hands I commend my spirit."*

[PAUSE •••]

That — is contentment. Not the contentment of a man who had everything he wanted. But the contentment of a man who had surrendered everything He had — to the One He trusted.

Today I want to preach to everybody in this room who is still fighting God for control of something He's already asked you to release.

This word is not for the comfortable. This word is for the ones who are tired of holding on — and haven't yet found the courage to let go.

📌 PREACHER'S TRAINING NOTE:
*Start quiet. Almost conversational. Don't come in hot — this text demands reverence. Let the congregation feel the weight of the cross before you say anything else. Eye contact on the opening line. Breathe before "Father, into thy hands." Let those words land like they're the first time anyone has ever heard them.*

II. SCRIPTURE READING + CONTEXT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

**Luke 23:46 (KJV)**
*"And when Jesus had cried with a loud voice, he said, Father, into thy hands I commend my spirit: and having said thus, he gave up the ghost."*

**CONTEXT:**
- **Book:** Luke — written by Luke the physician, for a Greek audience (Theophilus). Luke's Gospel emphasizes the full humanity of Jesus — His compassion, His prayer life, His care for the marginalized.
- **Chapter:** Luke 23 is the crucifixion narrative. Jesus has been arrested, tried, mocked, beaten, and nailed to a cross between two thieves.
- **Verse:** This is the seventh and final word from the cross. The word of completion. The word of release. Luke is the only Gospel writer who records these exact words — drawn from Psalm 31:5. Jesus dies quoting Scripture.

III. SERMON BODY — 3-POINT EXPOSITORY OUTLINE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

────────────────────────────────────────────────
POINT 1: Contentment Requires a Relationship
*"Father…"*
────────────────────────────────────────────────

**EXPOSITION:**
Before Jesus says anything else in this final breath — He establishes the address. Not "God." Not "LORD." Not "Almighty." He says *Father.*

In Greek, the word Luke records is **Pater** (Πατήρ) — the relational word. The intimate word. But scholars note that the cry Jesus likely uttered from the cross was in Aramaic — **Abba.** The daddy word. The word a child uses when they are not performing for a crowd — when they are simply, desperately, talking to the One they belong to.

🔤 EXPOSITORY INSIGHT:
- **Greek Word:** *Pater* (Πατήρ) — "Father, ancestor, the one from whom life originates"
- **Aramaic Undertone:** *Abba* — an intimate, familial term; not formal address but personal trust
- **Theological Significance:** Jesus does not cry out to God as Judge in this moment. He cries out to God as Father. This changes everything. Contentment is not available to a stranger. It flows from a relationship. You cannot release what you have into the hands of someone you don't trust.
- **Practical Reflection:** The reason many of us cannot find contentment is not a lack of discipline — it's a lack of intimacy. We know about God but we don't know Him well enough to trust Him with the things that matter most.

[PAUSE •••] [SLOW ↓]

**ILLUSTRATION / TESTIMONY BRIDGE:**
There's something about the word "Father" that changes the posture of the person speaking it.

A child lost in a crowded store doesn't call out for "the adult male who is responsible for my well-being." They cry out — *Daddy.* Not because they have it all figured out. Not because they know exactly where he is. But because they know he's looking for them too.

[TESTIMONY]

Some of us have been in this crowd — this mess, this confusion, this pain — and we've been so busy trying to find our way out that we forgot to just call out His name.

Not a prayer. Not a petition. Not a theological statement.

Just — Father.

[RISE ↑]

**DECLARATION LINE:**
*Contentment doesn't begin when your situation changes — it begins the moment you remember who your Father is.*

📌 PREACHER'S TRAINING NOTE:
*This first point should feel like a warm invitation, not a lecture. You're drawing people into intimacy with God. Let the word "Father" sit in the air. Say it slowly. Say it like it means something to you personally. If the 1997 testimony connects here — the moment you came back to the Father after running — this is the place to plant that seed.*

---

────────────────────────────────────────────────
POINT 2: Contentment Requires a Release
*"…into thy hands I commend my spirit…"*
────────────────────────────────────────────────

**EXPOSITION:**
The word *commend* is where this sermon lives.

In the Greek, Luke uses the word **paratithemai** (παρατίθεμαι) — which means to place something alongside, to deposit, to entrust for safekeeping. It is the word used when someone places a valuable item into the keeping of another. It is a banking term. A trust term. A word that assumes you will not see what you deposit again — but you are confident it is in good hands.

🔤 EXPOSITORY INSIGHT:
- **Greek Word:** *Paratithemai* (παρατίθεμαι) — "to set before, to deposit, to commit to the care of another"
- **Root:** *Para* (alongside) + *tithemi* (to place) — literally, to place something alongside someone for safekeeping
- **Historical Context:** This was a legal term in the ancient world. When a merchant left town, he would *paratithemai* his valuables — place them in trust — with a known, reliable person. There was no coming back to micromanage. It was a full release of ownership.
- **The Scripture Behind the Scripture:** Jesus is quoting Psalm 31:5 — *"Into thine hand I commit my spirit: thou hast redeemed me, O LORD God of truth."* This was a bedtime prayer every Jewish child learned. Jesus dies with a childhood prayer on His lips. He goes to sleep in the Father's hands.
- **Practical Reflection:** What you will not release, you will keep carrying. And the weight of carrying what was never yours to control is exactly what is stealing your contentment.

[PAUSE •••]

**ILLUSTRATION / TESTIMONY BRIDGE:**
When you make a deposit at the bank — you don't stand at the teller window all day watching your money to make sure it's still there.

You deposit it. You trust the institution. And you walk away.

[SLOW ↓]

But some of us have made a "deposit" to God — we've said *"Lord, I give this to You"* — and then we walked to the door, turned around, walked back to the window, asked for it back, went home, lay awake worrying about it, called a friend about it, Googled it, tried to fix it ourselves, made it worse, cried about it — and then Sunday morning we came back to church and said, *"Lord, I give this to You"* — again.

[PAUSE •••] [RISE ↑]

That is not a release. That is a rental.

Real contentment says — I put it in Your hands. And I left it there.

[RISE ↑]

Jesus — on the cross, in pain, about to die — made the ultimate deposit.
He put His *spirit* — the most precious thing He had — into His Father's hands.
And He did not take it back.

**DECLARATION LINE:**
*You will never experience contentment with what you are still trying to control.*

📌 PREACHER'S TRAINING NOTE:
*This is the theological center of the sermon — let it breathe. The "deposit/rental" illustration should get some laughter and then some conviction — ride that. The Greek word study on paratithemai is gold — explain it slowly, like you're unwrapping something. Don't rush through the word study. The congregation needs to feel the weight of what "commend" actually means before the declaration line lands.*

---

────────────────────────────────────────────────
POINT 3: Contentment Is an Act of Will, Not a Feeling
*"…and having said thus, he gave up the ghost."*
────────────────────────────────────────────────

**EXPOSITION:**
Luke adds a detail that every other Gospel records — but Luke's version carries the weight of a physician's precision. He says Jesus *gave up the ghost.* Not that He lost consciousness. Not that life was taken from Him. He *gave* it.

In Greek, Luke uses **exepneusen** (ἐξέπνευσεν) — from *ek* (out) and *pneuma* (breath/spirit) — He breathed out. He exhaled His life. This was a volitional act. Jesus did not die reluctantly. He died deliberately. Contentment was not the result of His circumstances improving. It was the result of a decision He made — to surrender on purpose.

🔤 EXPOSITORY INSIGHT:
- **Greek Word:** *Exepneusen* (ἐξέπνευσεν) — "he breathed out, he expired" — from *pneuma*, the word for spirit/breath
- **Theological Significance:** Jesus does not have His life taken — He lays it down (cf. John 10:18 — *"No one takes it from me, but I lay it down of my own accord."*). Contentment is always a choice. It is an act of the will, made in the middle of the worst moment, before the circumstances have resolved.
- **Historical Context:** Roman crucifixion victims typically died slowly over days. The fact that Jesus dies in hours — and does so with a declaration — astonished even the Roman centurion (Luke 23:47). Surrender does not look like weakness to a watching world. It looks like power they cannot explain.
- **Practical Reflection:** Contentment is not a feeling that arrives when things get better. It is a decision you make while things are still hard. You don't wait to feel at peace. You choose to release — and the peace follows.

[PAUSE •••]

**ILLUSTRATION / TESTIMONY BRIDGE:**
There is a difference between a man who falls and a man who kneels.

Both end up on the ground. But one got there by force — and one got there by choice.

[SLOW ↓]

Jesus knelt. In the hardest moment of human history — He knelt. He chose surrender. He chose release. He chose the Father's hands over His own.

[PAUSE •••]

And I want to ask you something this morning.

Is there something in your life right now that God has been asking you to release — and you keep falling instead of kneeling?

You keep getting knocked down by it instead of choosing to lay it down?

[RISE ↑]

The cross didn't make Jesus content.
The relationship made Him content.
The release made Him content.
The choice — to exhale everything He had into the hands of the Father —
That made Him content.

[CRESCENDO ↑↑↑]

**CADENCE LADDER — BUILD HERE:**

Level 1 (Conversational):
*"Contentment is a choice."*

Level 2 (Teaching):
*"It is a choice you make before the situation resolves."*

Level 3 (Passionate Declaration):
*"Jesus made that choice — nailed to a cross — with nothing left in His hands but the decision to trust His Father."*

Level 4 (Full Crescendo):
*"AND IF JESUS COULD FIND CONTENTMENT ON A CROSS — TELL ME WHY YOU AND I CAN'T FIND IT IN THE MIDDLE OF OUR SITUATION — WHEN WE HAVE THE SAME FATHER — AND THE SAME SPIRIT — AND THE SAME PROMISE — THAT HE IS ABLE TO KEEP WHAT WE COMMIT TO HIM!"*

[LET THEM RESPOND]

📌 PREACHER'S TRAINING NOTE:
*This is where you preach. Use the Cadence Ladder deliberately — don't jump to Level 4 early. Let Level 1 and 2 breathe. By the time you hit Level 4, the congregation should already be leaning in. The "falling vs. kneeling" illustration is visceral — let it sit. The rhetorical question "is there something God has been asking you to release?" is not for you to answer — let them sit with it. That silence is the Spirit working.*

IV. CLOSE (2 minutes)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[SLOW ↓] [WHISPER]

I want to leave you with this.

Jesus had every reason to be bitter. Every reason to demand relief. Every reason to hold on. But at the end — at the very end — He opened His hands.

He opened His hands — and let go.

[PAUSE •••]

ONE FINAL TRUTH:
*Contentment is not the absence of pain. It is the presence of trust — deep enough to release what you love most into the hands of a Father who loves you more.*

[PAUSE •••]

ONE MEMORABLE LINE:
*"You were never meant to hold it. You were meant to hand it over."*

[PAUSE •••]

ONE INVITATION / CHALLENGE:
There is something you walked in here carrying today that God is not asking you to figure out. He is asking you to release it. Right now — before you leave this room — before you pick your phone back up — before you go back to trying to fix what only He can fix —

Open your hands.

Say it out loud if you have to.

*"Father — into Your hands — I commend this."*

And mean it.

[WHISPER] [SLOW ↓]

CLOSING LINE (Memorize):
*"The most content person in the room is not the one with the least problems — it is the one who has learned to open their hands."*

V. PREACHER'S STUDY JOURNAL
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

**PERSONAL REFLECTION PROMPTS:**
1. What is the thing in your own life right now that you have "committed to God" but taken back? How does this sermon challenge you personally before you preach it to others?
2. When in your own journey have you experienced the difference between falling and kneeling — between having something taken versus choosing to lay it down? How does that testimony live in this text?
3. Jesus quotes Psalm 31:5 — a childhood prayer — in His final breath. What prayers, scriptures, or faith anchors do you return to in your hardest moments? How does that inform how you preach this text?
4. This sermon asks the congregation to release something specific. Before you stand in the pulpit — what do YOU need to release? Preach out of your surrender, not just your preparation.

**CROSS-REFERENCES FOR DEEPER STUDY:**
- **Psalm 31:5** — *"Into thine hand I commit my spirit: thou hast redeemed me, O LORD God of truth."* — The Old Testament source text Jesus quotes. Study the full Psalm — David wrote it in the middle of distress, not comfort.
- **John 10:17–18** — *"I lay down my life… No one takes it from me, but I lay it down of my own accord."* — The voluntary nature of Jesus' surrender. Contentment is always chosen.
- **Philippians 4:11–13** — *"I have learned, in whatsoever state I am, therewith to be content."* — Paul's contentment is not a personality trait — it is a learned discipline. Connects directly to the theme.
- **1 Peter 5:7** — *"Casting all your care upon him; for he careth for you."* — The word *casting* in Greek (*epiripsantes*) means to throw with force — not a gentle offering, but a decisive release.
- **Romans 8:15** — *"For ye have not received the spirit of bondage again to fear; but ye have received the Spirit of adoption, whereby we cry, Abba, Father."* — The Abba connection. We have the same access to the Father that Jesus demonstrated on the cross.

**PRAYER BEFORE YOU PREACH:**

*Father — before I open my mouth to speak this word, I open my hands to live it.*

*You know what I am carrying. You know what I have not fully surrendered. Let this sermon do its work in me before it does its work in them.*

*Give me the courage to preach from a place of release — not from a place of theory. Let the congregation feel in my voice that this is not just something I studied — it is something I have lived.*

*And when I speak the words "into thy hands" — let every person in that room feel the invitation to do what Jesus did — to trust You enough to let go.*

*In Jesus' name. Amen.*
